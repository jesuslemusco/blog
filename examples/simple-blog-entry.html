5 Common Mistakes in CI/CD Pipelines

Published on July 15, 2024 by Jesus Lemus

Continuous Integration and Continuous Delivery (CI/CD) have become the backbone of modern software development, enabling teams to ship features faster and more reliably. However, setting up a truly effective pipeline is more than just connecting a few tools. Many teams fall into common traps that negate the benefits of automation. Here are five of the most common mistakes I see and how to fix them.

1. Overly Complex and Monolithic Pipelines

One of the first mistakes teams make is building a single, monolithic pipeline that tries to do everything: build, test, scan, deploy to staging, run integration tests, and deploy to production. This creates a slow, brittle process where a single failure can halt the entire delivery chain for hours.

2. Ignoring Pipeline Security

Pipelines are a prime target for attackers. They often have access to sensitive credentials, source code, and production environments. Yet, many teams leave secrets in plain text, use overly permissive access roles, or fail to scan their dependencies for vulnerabilities.

Solution: Treat your pipeline's security as seriously as your application's. Use a secrets management tool like HashiCorp Vault or AWS Secrets Manager. Implement the principle of least privilege for pipeline roles, and integrate security scanning tools (SAST, DAST, and dependency scanning) directly into your pipeline. For example, here's a snippet from a GitHub Actions workflow that uses a security scanner:

- name: Scan for vulnerabilities
  uses: actions/security-scanner@v1.0
  with:
    path: './src'
    level: 'high'

3. Flaky and Unreliable Tests

A CI/CD pipeline is only as reliable as its tests. If your tests are flaky—sometimes passing and sometimes failing without any code changes—developers will quickly lose trust in the pipeline. They'll start ignoring failures or, worse, disabling the tests altogether.

"A flaky test suite erodes trust and defeats the purpose of automation. When a test fails, it should be a meaningful signal, not noise."
— A Wise Engineer

Invest time in writing stable, deterministic tests. Isolate tests from external dependencies using mocking, and ensure your test environment is consistent and reproducible. When a flaky test is identified, make it a priority to fix it.

4. Lack of Observability

When a pipeline fails, how quickly can you diagnose the problem? Many teams have pipelines that are black boxes. They might get a simple "build failed" notification, but then have to dig through thousands of lines of logs to find the root cause.

Your pipeline needs good observability. Use tools that provide clear visualizations of your pipeline's stages. Add structured logging to your build scripts, and send metrics and alerts to a centralized monitoring system. The goal is to make failures obvious and easy to debug.

5. Treating the Pipeline as a "Set and Forget" System

Finally, a CI/CD pipeline is not a one-time setup. It's a product that needs to be maintained and improved over time. Technologies change, new tools emerge, and your team's needs will evolve. A pipeline that was state-of-the-art two years ago might be a bottleneck today.

Assign ownership of the pipeline to a specific team or individual. Regularly review its performance, and look for opportunities to simplify, speed up, or secure it. Continuous improvement should apply not just to your application code, but to the systems that deliver it as well.
